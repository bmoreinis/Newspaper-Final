Racism in the education system


The Oklahoma government passed a bill that restricts the teaching of critical race theoryat any schools, this law also includes banning many of the historical lessons we learned as kids. There were already so many gapes in the things we were taught, they sugar coated so much of the true racism in our country's history. Now they are fully getting rid of this country’s past, a heartbreaking story that people already didn’t know enough about. Kevin Stitt signed a bill into law on Friday barring grade schools from teaching lessons about race or sex that may make students uncomfortable.


Many people have spoken up on their opinions about this new law, posting on social media, or speaking up through books or interviews. “It’s a law that protects white students from white fragility by banning any subject that forces them to think critically about American racism past, present or future,” wrote Zack Linly, “This isn’t about education, it’s about racism…. Banning [critical race theory] does just as much to further the divide as teaching it apparently does. Black feelings are being disregarded while white feelings are being catered to.”


Paula Lewis, the chairwoman of the Oklahoma City School Board, called HB 1775 “an outright racist and oppressive piece of legislation” on Twitter. “I am appalled at the flagrant attempt to erase factual, incomprehensible history that has occurred in the United States,” Lewis wrote in a Twitter thread. “Our history as a country and as a state, if told accurately, is uncomfortable and should be heartbreaking for Americans that look like me, white.”

“The intention of the bill clearly aims to limit teaching the racial implications of America’s history. The bill serves no purpose than to fuel the racism and denial that afflicts our communities and our nation,” writes the commission. “It is a sad day and a stain on Oklahoma.” “The term critical race theory is being used by Republicans in a loose way to capture all sorts of critical thought about the histories and legacies of racism in this country,” Amna Akbar, an associate law professor at Ohio State University, told The Hill. “It’s a bogeyman that they’re constructing around critical attention to the history of the country.”

Racism in the Beauty Industry

So many of today’s “aesthetics” and trends, have internalized racism that some may realize and choose to ignore, may not realize, and the growing percentage of people who speak up for those who aren’t white. White people don’t even realize the hypocrisy in some of the things they tend to do. They curl their hair to have beautiful luscious curls that african americans have, but tell those same african americans to “comb a brush through their hair.” They get plastic surgery to have full lips like many african americans, but ask them if a plastic surgeon blouched their surgery up, as a “joke.” They get plastic surgery to get the natural curves african americans have, then have the audacity to say that they probably did the same and got plastic surgery. I can’t believe that some people could look someone in the eyes and say something racist when they do some of these things.


Now as far as makeup goes, the foundation lines are one of the best examples of racism in the makeup industry. In so many famous foundation lines they have almost 10-20 color options for lighter skin toned people, while for darker colored people are stuck with around 3 options. This has caused some people to even use harmful and toxic skin lightening creams, and many other products. So many people with darker skin tones can’t seem to find a foundation color that actually matches their skin. This issue also occurs in contour, some of the colors quite literally blend into their skin when they are meant to seem like shadows.


Nevermind the limited options for darker skinned followers, they also don’t have enough representation. I never see darker skinned people who model for big companies, even if there are lines with products for black people they rarely ever have models that are darker toned to show them off. In advertisements for curly hair products african american’s natural 4c hair are instead wavy because those are more “ideal” curls. In itself that is a whole racist concept, but the fact that people advertise those products as things for 4c curls is disrespectful for so many reasons.
